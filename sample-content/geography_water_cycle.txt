LESSON: THE WATER CYCLE
Subject: Geography / Environmental Science | Grade: 6-7 | Duration: 25 minutes

INTRODUCTION
=============
The water cycle (also called the hydrological cycle) is the continuous movement of water 
through the Earth's systems — from the oceans to the sky and back again. It has no 
beginning and no end; it cycles endlessly.

THE FOUR MAIN PROCESSES
=========================

PROCESS 1: EVAPORATION
  What happens: Liquid water turns into water vapour (gas) and rises into the atmosphere.
  Where it happens: Oceans, lakes, rivers, puddles — any water surface.
  What causes it: HEAT from the sun provides energy for water molecules to escape.
  
  Scale: The oceans supply about 90% of all water vapour in the atmosphere.
  Rate: 502,800 km³ of water evaporates from oceans every year.

PROCESS 2: TRANSPIRATION
  What happens: Plants release water vapour through tiny holes in their leaves called 
  STOMATA. This is similar to evaporation but from plants.
  
  EVAPOTRANSPIRATION = Evaporation + Transpiration combined
  
  Fun fact: A single large oak tree releases about 200 litres of water per day!
  Amazon rainforest: Releases so much water it creates its own weather patterns.

PROCESS 3: CONDENSATION
  What happens: Water vapour cools as it rises higher in the atmosphere. When it cools 
  enough, it turns back into tiny liquid water droplets around dust particles.
  These droplets cluster together to form CLOUDS.
  
  Dew Point: The temperature at which air becomes saturated with water vapour 
  and condensation begins.
  
  Types of clouds formed:
  • CUMULUS   - Fluffy, white, like cotton wool. Found at mid-level.
  • STRATUS   - Flat, grey layers. Bring drizzle and overcast skies.
  • CIRRUS    - High, thin, wispy streaks of ice crystals.
  • CUMULONIMBUS - Huge, towering storm clouds. Bring heavy rain and thunder.

PROCESS 4: PRECIPITATION
  What happens: When water droplets in clouds combine and become too heavy, they fall 
  back to Earth's surface.
  
  Forms of precipitation:
  • Rain    - Liquid water droplets (>0°C)
  • Snow    - Ice crystals that fall when temperature is below 0°C throughout
  • Sleet   - Rain that partially freezes (mix of rain and snow)
  • Hail    - Balls of ice formed by repeated freezing in storm clouds

ADDITIONAL PROCESSES
======================

SURFACE RUNOFF
  After precipitation, water flows over land surfaces into rivers and streams,
  eventually returning to the ocean. Amount depends on:
  - Slope of land (steeper = faster runoff)
  - Rock/soil type (impermeable rock = more runoff)
  - Vegetation cover (plants slow runoff and increase absorption)

INFILTRATION & GROUNDWATER
  Water soaks into the soil and rock layers below.
  - INFILTRATION: Movement of water into the soil
  - PERCOLATION: Water moves deeper into rock layers (aquifers)
  - GROUNDWATER: Water stored in underground rock. This feeds springs and wells.
  - Groundwater can stay underground for hundreds or thousands of years!

THE WATER CYCLE DIAGRAM (described spatially):
===============================================
Imagine a cross-section landscape from left to right:

  LEFT: Ocean (large body of water at ground level)
    ↑ Evaporation arrows rising upward from ocean surface
    
  CENTER-LEFT: Rising water vapour forms clouds at altitude
    ↑ Clouds move inland (right) driven by wind
    
  CENTER: Mountain range rises from ground
    ↓ Heavy precipitation falls on mountain slopes
    
  CENTER-RIGHT: Precipitation becomes:
    → Surface runoff (rivers flowing right back toward ocean)
    ↓ Infiltration (water sinking into ground)
    
  RIGHT: Rivers/streams meet the ocean, cycle begins again.
  
  UNDERGROUND: Groundwater slowly flows from mountain to ocean
               through permeable rock layers (aquifers).

GLOBAL WATER DISTRIBUTION
===========================
Total water on Earth: ~1.4 billion km³

  97.5% = Salt water (oceans) - NOT directly drinkable
   2.5% = Fresh water
        Of that 2.5%:
        68.7% = Locked in glaciers and ice caps
        30.1% = Groundwater
         0.9% = Other (soil moisture, atmosphere, swamps)
         0.3% = Surface freshwater (rivers, lakes) ← ONLY THIS IS EASILY ACCESSIBLE

The water cycle is what REPLENISHES this accessible freshwater supply continuously.

CLIMATE CHANGE AND THE WATER CYCLE
=====================================
Global warming is affecting the water cycle:
• Higher temperatures → MORE evaporation → More water vapour in atmosphere
• More water vapour → More intense storms and heavier rainfall events
• Glaciers melting → Changes in river flows seasonally
• Some regions getting WETTER, others getting DRIER
• Rising sea levels from glacier melt changes coastal water movement

KEY VOCABULARY
===============
- Evaporation: Liquid water → water vapour due to heat
- Condensation: Water vapour → liquid water droplets (clouds)
- Precipitation: Water falling from clouds (rain, snow, sleet, hail)
- Transpiration: Water vapour released by plants
- Infiltration: Water soaking into soil
- Groundwater: Water stored underground in rock layers (aquifers)
- Runoff: Water flowing over land surface into rivers
- Hydrological cycle: Another name for the water cycle
